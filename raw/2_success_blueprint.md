# 2. Strategic model: do not choose funnel OR editor

This is the key answer to your fear.

## Your fear

You’re afraid the editor won’t convert as much as a classic funnel.

That fear is valid.

A pure editor usually converts worse than a strong guided funnel because:

* more choices
* more blank-state anxiety
* less immediate momentum
* weaker psychological commitment

But a pure funnel also has limitations:

* shallow long-term utility
* weak retention
* repetitive workflows
* poor professional control
* harder to differentiate deeply

## The correct answer

Do **not** choose one.

Build a **hybrid system**:

### Layer 1: Guided activation funnel

This is for:

* cold traffic
* one-shot users
* low-confidence users
* mobile/quick users
* people who just want a fast result

### Layer 2: Structured editor workspace

This is for:

* refinement
* repeated applications
* imports
* deeper control
* long-term users
* account expansion

### Principle

**The funnel creates commitment.
The editor creates retention and superiority.**

That is the winning model.

# 8. ResumeLab popup editing vs your skeleton system

* feels tangible
* closer to the final product
* beginner-friendly in the short term

### Defects

## Your skeleton + live preview model

### Advantages

* template-independent editing
* scalable mental model
* better for retention
* better for content reuse
* better for professional users
* better for system thinking

### Defects

* less immediately seductive
* can feel “builder-like” or abstract
* requires good mapping between editor and preview

## Best answer

**Your model is better as the core system.**

* structured editor remains primary
* live preview always visible
* preview blocks are clickable
* clicking preview highlights the matching structured section
* tiny contextual editing entry points exist in preview
* important doc-level parts can open focused sheets/modals

That gets you the best of both worlds.

# 11. AI product model — what ideal looks like

This is one of the most important parts.

Your AI should operate in **three modes**.

## Mode 1 — Draft AI

Used to create initial document blocks.
Needs:

* strong guardrails
* job awareness
* anti-generic writing
* structure awareness
* tone consistency

## Mode 2 — Assist AI

Used inside workflow:

* rewrite
* shorten
* clarify
* fix grammar
* improve ATS alignment
* make more formal / warmer
* extract bullets
* convert paragraph to bullets
* convert bullets to paragraph

This is where your floating toolbar belongs.

## Mode 3 — Suggestion AI

Used only when choices help:

* give 2–4 strong variants
* ranked
* visibly distinct
* never 10 paragraphs of junk

This is where I disagree with a simplistic assumption.

You are right that:

* too many suggestions are bad
* weak variant lists make AI feel dumb
* forcing users to read 10 bad options is anti-UX

But the answer is not “never show suggestions.”

The answer is:

## Show **small, curated suggestion sets**

Examples:

* “pick 1 of 3 stronger summaries”
* “pick 1 of 2 opening lines”
* “choose compact / persuasive / formal rewrite”
* “choose better verb set”

That is better than:

* one opaque rewrite with no comparison
* or ten garbage options

## My blunt view

If the AI can only produce one version and user must trust it blindly, that can feel efficient at first, but it creates hidden distrust.

Best pattern:

* **default = one strong rewrite**
* plus optional “show alternatives”
* alternatives capped at 2–3
* visibly different, not superficial

That is the sweet spot.

# 14. Parsing / import system — this must become a core product priority

This is not a side issue.

This is central.

Because a premium workflow with unreliable parsing feels fake.

## Ideal import pipeline

### Stage 1 — Raw ingest

Take PDF / DOCX / pasted content / LinkedIn import.

### Stage 2 — Structural normalization

Detect:

* profile lines
* summary
* roles
* dates
* bullet blocks
* education
* skills
* certifications
* languages

### Stage 3 — Confidence scoring

Each extracted block gets a confidence score.

### Stage 4 — Review queue

If confidence is low, do not silently place it.
Surface:

* “Review 3 imported blocks”
* “We’re not sure where these belong”
* “Fix before generating”

### Stage 5 — Cleanup AI

Normalize:

* weird glyphs
* merged lines
* duplicate fragments
* bad bullet formatting
* date cleanup
* capitalization cleanup

### Stage 6 — User confirmation

Very fast review layer.

## Why ResumeLab feels stronger

Probably not because parsing is magically perfect.
Probably because:

* cleanup is better hidden
* structure is constrained
* review burden is lower
* backend correction is always-on

You should move in that direction.

## Big rule

**Never dump uncertain parse results directly into final sections without review.**

That is one of the fastest ways to kill trust.
